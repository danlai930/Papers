Satellite imagery‑based Airbus ship localization and detection using deep learning‑based approaches

Abstract

Due to the increasing ship trafficking, illegal fishing, cargo crimes, drug crimes, identifying people in the sea, shipwrecked etc., it is essential to detect ships to monitor and control maritime crime. Researchers have been working constantly and facing challenges to automatically detect ships, mainly in remote sensing, which has shown a great impact in providing safety, detecting illegal trade, pollution, and oil slick monitoring. In this paper, 192,555 satellite images of ships have been used to train the deep learning models such as customized CNN, ResNet152V2, InceptionResNetV2, MobileNetV2, NasNetLarge, DenseNet201, and EfficientNetB7. The dataset has been first pre-processed to remove any noisy signals before being graphically represented using exploratory data analysis to study the pixel intensities of data. Later, feature extraction techniques such as image masking, image augmentation, and contour features are applied to create bounding boxes corresponding to the detected ship’s localizing coordinates. The models are further evaluated using the ship detected and ship not detected classes. It has been discovered that customized CNN achieved the highest precision by 96.16 as well as 95.64 and F1 score value by 99.89 and 95.99 in terms of both the classes, respectively. In contrast, DenseNet201 had the highest recall 95.46, and 93.45, for the ship, detected and not detected classes, respectively).


Keywords Airbus ship detection · CNN · EfficientNetB7 · Bounding boxes · Image masking · Image augmentation

    It has been observed that the conventional techniques for identifying and locating the ships in satellite imagery were labor and time-intensive.

    On the other hand, RGB optical imagery-based ship detection employs machine learning and deep learning techniques to detect ships in visible light-captured images. This strategy is based on the fact that ships have distinct visual characteristics, such as size, shape, and color that can be detected by computer vision algorithms. RGB (Red, Green, Blue) images consist of three color channels representing the intensity of red, green, and blue light in each pixel. These channels may be combined to produce a full-color image that appears realistic to the human eye. RGB imagery can be used to identify ships based on their visual characteristics, such as the color of the hull or the shape of the vessel, in the context of ship detection. Several machine learning and deep learning techniques can be utilised for RGB-based ship detection, including object detection algorithms such as YOLO and Faster R-CNN and semantic segmentation techniques such as UNet and Mask R-CNN [6]. These algorithms can be trained on labelled RGB image datasets in which ships are annotated and identified using bounding boxes or segmentation masks. Once the algorithm has been trained, it can be used to automatically detect ships in new images by comparing the image’s color and texture features to those learned during training. The algorithm’s output can be a collection of bounding boxes or segmentation masks that highlight the location of the ships in the image. This data can be utilised for numerous purposes, including maritime surveillance, environmental monitoring, and search and rescue operations [7].

    Convolutional neural networks (CNN) are a recent technology that has seen an increase in successful implementation.


    Such networks are used to carry out various image processing tasks, including super-resolution, semantic segmentation, and object detection. Deep Learning (DL) techniques, particularly DCNN, which can extract trustworthy features that facilitate ship detection and localization automatically, have been the subject of numerous research studies. Researchers applied R-DFPN technique to detect the ship which was based on multi scale detection network [8] and proposed localization estimation detector (LEDet) [9] to generate localization score of model for predicting the position of ship. Sea-Land Segmentation-based Convolutional Neural Network (SLS-CNN) was used in [10] that brought together the saliency computation, SLS-CNN detector, and corner features for detecting the ships. As seen in Fig. 2, these techniques were also referred to as horizontal region detection.

    Apart from this, machine and deep learning algorithms have become popular for acoustic source localization as well as sea floor classification. Researchers such as Niu et al. [11] applied multiple learning algorithms such as random forest (RF), support vector machine (SVM), and feed forward neural network to locate the ship in shallow water of 152 m depth by training the system with propagation relationship which was received from the acoustic pressure on a vertical array and the known ship GPS. Later, they worked for 600 m depth deep water to locate the position of the ship in Santa Barbara Channel Experiment with limited environmental information using classical matched field processing (MFP) [12]. Keeping this drawback in view, Wang et al. [13] applied pre trained deep learning models to develop a scalable system instead of MFP technique to localize ship acoustic source in a shallow water environment along with multi element hydrophone array data. As a result, it can be said that a deep learning approach has had a lot of success

    in detecting and localizing the ships by processing ship image data [14].

    Deep transfer learning involves fine-tuning pre-trained deep neural networks for a specific task, such as ship detection in water, using pre-trained deep neural networks. This strategy can utilise the knowledge gained from a large amount of data in other domains, such as object recognition in images, to enhance the precision of ship detection in water. In addition, deep transfer learning can reduce the need for manual feature engineering because pre-trained networks can automatically learn features from the data. This can increase the effectiveness of ship detection in water by reducing the time and resources required for feature engineering.

    The purpose of detecting ships in water using deep transfer learning is to enhance the precision, efficiency, and effectiveness of maritime surveillance for various applications. Hence, in this paper, the aim of the paper is to detect the presence or the absence of the ship in the water for which various deep transfer learning models such as ResNet152V2, InceptionResNetV2, MobileNetV2, NasNet-Large, DenseNet201, and EfficientNetB7 have been applied along with the proposed CNN model. The models have been assessed based on several factors, including accuracy, loss, precision, F1 score, RMSE, and recall. When the model was applied to a testing dataset, proposed CNN model generated the highest accuracy by 99.80%, and InceptionResNetV2 computed the best RMSE (root mean square error) and loss values by 0.5824 and 0.3992, respectively. During the training phase, CNN calculated the best accuracy, loss, and RMSE values by 99.24%, 0.0266, and 0.1630.

  2. Related work‌

    As previously stated, researchers have left no stone unturned in their efforts to improve the performance of their system using artificial intelligence. To detect the ship through satellite images, Chen et al. [15] used various machine and deep learning methods such as RF, k-nearest neighbor, Naive Bayes, linear discriminant analysis, SVM, and CNN. They also used feature extraction techniques to find global feature descriptors of an image, such as Hu Moments and Histogram of Oriented Gradients. Patel et al. [5] concentrated on developing an automatic ship detection (ASD) approach to assess the Airbus ship dataset using DL methods. To accomplish this, they used and compared various versions of YOLO, such as YOLOv4, YOLOv3, and YOLOv5, on 40k satellite images to detect ships. Similarly, Lou et al. [16] proposed a solution to the problem of obtaining and labelling data for ship detection in Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) images. The network for the transfer of knowledge produced pseudo-SAR images with the same spatial characteristics as labelled optical images and the same characteristics as SAR images. Even when there were no labelled SAR images available during training, these pseudo-SAR images improved the accuracy of detection models based on convolutional neural networks. The framework was evaluated on two datasets, and the results demonstrated that the generated pseudo-SAR images improved the accuracy of ship detection and reduced the probability of missing detections or false positives, even in complex backgrounds.

    Sharma et al. [17] identified ships using object detection techniques such as You Only Look Once (YOLO) v3, EfficientDet-D2, RetinaNet152, YOLO v4, and Faster-RCNN, as well as the K-means + + clustering algorithm, which outperformed the accuracy, speed, and efficiency. Mehran et al. [18] identified ships in satellite images using InceptionResnetV2. Utilizing and evaluating multiple Deep Learning models. The “Ships in Satellite Imagery” dataset from Kaggle was used to detect ships in images. The

    outcomes demonstrated that the use of transfer learning and data enhancement resulted in successful ship detection with an accuracy rate exceeding 99.9%. In addition, experimenting with different deep learning models for this task yielded accurate results in less time. Hashi et al. [19] proposed a robust model for detecting ships that used learning models like the Naive Bayes, decision tree, RF, and CNN. Their proposed model’s importance was developing a novel method for detecting ships. In addition, during the pre-processing of the images, they used other techniques such as feature extraction, block division, and color feature.

    Li et al. [20] used an approach for the detection and classification of the ships practically using an optical remote sensing images as well as their corresponding extracted features and statistical data. Their paper also summarized datasets that were available publically as a benchmarks to present the objectivity of ship identification and classification methods. Karki and Kulkarni [21] conducted experiments on satellite-based image datasets to evaluate various loss functions and encoders for the UNET model on the basis of their segmentation results that had been applied on the test dataset. According to the researchers, the modes were robust and performed well even with complex backgrounds such as clouds, waves, etc. Ramesh et al. [22] proposed a ship identification and classification model that combined YOLT and inception architecture. They trained the models on an airbus ship detection dataset, and their accuracy was much higher than that of a traditional image classifier.

    Zhang et al. [23] introduced SCNN, a novel method for detecting ships using convolutional neural networks (CNNs). In addition to proposals designed specifically for ship models, their method also included an improved saliency detection method. The researchers created two ship models the “||” ship body model and the “V” ship head model in order to identify ship from the line segments that had been extracted from test images. These two types of ship proposals were then fed into a trained CNN for detection effectiveness and reliability.

    Using a combination of the maximum symmetric surround saliency detection algorithm and a cellular automaton dynamic evolution model, Wang et al. [24] focused in on specific regions identified by a combination of these two techniques. Next, they created an eigenvector independent of the target ship’s size by combining the shape feature with the ship histogram of oriented gradient feature (S-HOG). They subsequently utilised an AdaBoost classifier to identify the target. Experiments demonstrated that their proposed method had a detection accuracy of greater than 96%. Smith et al. [25] stated that the detection of ships using deep learning in satellite images could be challenging due to the presence of clouds, land, and man-made objects

    that produce false positives. Consequently, the precision of accurately classifying ships was typically limited. In their study, they examined the relationship between cloud-based solutions’ customization methods, training times, costs, and class accuracy rates. A customised U-Net model achieved 92% accuracy on a validation dataset and 68% accuracy on a target dataset, according to their findings.

    Louropoulos et al. [6] initially divided the image into multiple segments and fed each segment through a CNN in order to locate the ship. However, this required considerable time to execute a lengthy algorithm. To circumvent this problem, the YOLO technique was implemented, which utilised a single Convolutional Neural Network for both object detection and localization. Although YOLO significantly decreased execution time, it significantly decreased prediction accuracy. As a solution, an end-to-end strategy incorporating both approaches was developed. YOLO was used as a region proposal algorithm, and the threshold was fine-tuned to improve prediction accuracy. The proposed regions were then submitted to a specialised CNN for classification (Table 1).

    2. Pre‑processing


      All the images in the dataset (Fig. 5) were pre-processed to improve their quality so that the model could correctly train and predict them. Initially, the images were resized to 256 × 256 pixels, and two morphological operations, erosion and dilation were used to remove and add pixels to the object’s boundaries. The number of pixels added or removed from an image’s objects is determined by the size and shape of the structuring element used to process the image. On the other hand, it is also important to mention their impact and consequences. Here are some observations that can be made. Erosion can remove fine details and structures from an image, including small objects, noise, and thin lines. However, it can also result in the loss of crucial information, such as the edges and boundaries of objects. Although dilation can add pixels to an object, making it thicker or larger, it cannot alter the object’s shape. Additionally, it can fill in spaces between objects and close small holes in an image. Nonetheless, if the structuring element is too large, it can introduce noise or blur edges. Moreover, both erosion and dilation are irreversible operations, which mean that the original image cannot be reconstructed from the eroded or dilated image. Before applying these operations, it is crucial to carefully consider the choice of structuring element

      1. CNN


        CNN’s are a type of Deep Neural Network that can recognize and classify specific features in images and are widely used for image analysis. The architecture of CNN as shown in Fig. 8 comprises of two main components i.e. convolution tool and fully connected layer. In convo layer, the convolution tool identifies the multiple features of an image which are further used for feature extraction while as in fully connected layer, the output nodes predict the class of the input image based on its features that have been extracted in the previous stage [30].

        In CNN, Convolution is a mathematical operation used to extract features from data inputs, such as images. Mathematically, convolution entails sliding a filter (also known as a kernel or a window) over the input data and calculating the dot product between the filter and the portion of the input data that is currently under the filter. This operation produces a new feature map that indicates the presence of a specific feature in the input data.

        Formally, consider 2D input data or an image with H x W dimensions and a 2D filter or kernel with K x K dimensions.

        The convolution operation consists of sliding the filter across the input data and computing the dot product between the filter and the corresponding portion of the input data. Table 3 shows the parameters of customized CNN architecture which has been proposed in this research paper.

  5. Conclusion‌

The paper used multiple pre-trained models, including ResNet152V2, InceptionResNetV2, MobileNetV2, NasNetLarge, DenseNet201, and EfficientNetB7, along with the proposed CNN model for detecting the presence and absence of ships in the water on the Airbus ship detection dataset. Techniques such as computing morphological values of contour features, image masking,

and generating bounding boxes around the extracted feature based on its coordinates in the water were used to extract the region of interest. The models mentioned above were further evaluated based on their performance metrics such as precision, loss, recall, accuracy, as well as RMSE. It was discovered that customized CNN model obtained the best values during the training phase. At the same time, CNN also shared the best accuracy during the testing phase.

While conducting the research, specific issues were encountered, such as how image pre-processing can be improved to remove noise so that our model can locate every ship in the water (particularly those not seen or are at a farther location). Along with this, the system should incorporate techniques for detecting false alarms to improve the prediction accuracy of detecting ships in water. In addition to this, the limited resolution of satellite images can make it difficult to distinguish small ships from background noise or other objects. This can lead to missed detections or false-positive results. Ships can vary in size, shape, and color, as well as be partially obscured by other objects in an image. These variations can make it difficult for the CNN model to accurately identify ships. Clouds, waves, and other vessels can complicate and clutter the background of satellite images. This can make it challenging for the CNN model to distinguish ships from the background. In satellite images, the number of ships is typically much smaller than the number of non-ship objects. This class imbalance may cause CNN model predictions to be skewed, resulting in decreased accuracy. All these challenges should be considered in future and the researchers should also investigate the rapid detection of ship targets in SAR images with fewer omissions of extremely small ships.
